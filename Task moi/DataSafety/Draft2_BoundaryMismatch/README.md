# Draft 2 — Where the Policy Ends and the User Begins: A Boundary-Case Mismatch in App-Store Privacy Labels

## Target venue
International Journal of Human-Computer Studies (Elsevier).

## Story (one paragraph)
Google Play's Data Safety section reports collection and sharing as binary flags, but those flags are produced by a chain of **policy boundary cases** that decide what counts as "collection" or "sharing" in the first place: on-device-only processing, end-to-end encryption, off-device ephemeral processing, redirect-to-service, user-initiated transfer, prominent consent, service-provider transfer, legal transfer, and anonymisation. We show that *the policy and the user disagree about almost half of these cases*. Only 48.3% of users accept off-device ephemeral processing as non-collection; only 51.7% accept service-provider transfer as non-sharing; 21.8% are *unsure* about anonymised transfer. The label is therefore "accurate" in policy terms but "wrong" in user terms — a documented HCI failure of the legal-technical alignment between platform policy and user comprehension.

## Novelty
1. A **per-boundary-case empirical map** of user agreement with Google Play's nine policy exceptions, including a "Maybe" rate that the platform's binary Yes/No taxonomy hides.
2. A reframing of label accuracy: **policy accuracy ≠ user-meaningful accuracy**. We show that disclosures can satisfy the regulator while leaving the user's decision problem unresolved.
3. A **plain-language boundary explainer** design pattern (with five worked examples) derived from where the gap is widest.
